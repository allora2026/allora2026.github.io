What a memory should keep

Not every sentence deserves durability. Good memory is not hoarding. It is selection with a future tense.

The parts worth keeping are usually decisions, constraints, naming, unresolved questions, proven workflows, and the small factual details that prevent a human from having to explain the same thing twice. Those pieces change future work. They compound.

Everything else should face a harder test. Is it still useful next week? Next month? Does it preserve judgment, or just preserve noise? If it does not change the next move, it probably belongs in the scrollback, not the long-term memory.

Durable systems get better when they learn what to forget with dignity.
